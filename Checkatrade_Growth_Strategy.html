Checkatrade Growth Strategy Memo

Executive Summary

Checkatrade should not try to win the next phase of the market by being a better directory. It should win by becoming the managed operating layer between homeowners and trades.

The core business still matters. It funds the transition and should be improved, not managed down. But the long-term source of defensibility will shift away from traffic and leads alone and toward four strategic bets:

  • Go deeper with supply by becoming the operating system for tradespeople, not just a source of leads.
  • Move up the marketplace management stack by using Express and adjacent models to shift from introductions toward trusted outcomes.
  • Build AI natively into the marketplace to improve scoping, matching, routing, and time-to-value.
  • Increase customer engagement by building the operating system homeowners use to manage their household over time.

The current data points to three immediate issues: demand likely needs to grow to hit plan, hire rate is too low and deteriorating, and the app is not yet creating a meaningfully better experience than web. That means the near-term priority is not simply more scale. It is to improve demand quality, improve BO-to-hire conversion, and make the app valuable enough to become the default surface for high-intent users.

For the core business, I would focus on three initiatives:

  • rebuild onboarding and intake as an AI-native problem-to-plan flow to improve BO quality
  • improve matching and workflow routing to increase hire rate
  • once the app proves superior conversion, retention, and LTV, shift more high-intent users from web into the app

For Express, I would treat it as the wedge into a more managed marketplace and launch supply-first within existing demand pools, starting in a small number of dense cities and digestible categories. Marketing capital should be allocated with discipline: first to owned demand and routing from the core business, then to organic and brand, then to selected trust-led pilots, with paid scaled only where supply readiness and payback are proven.

Organizationally, I would run Checkatrade as one integrated system, not two businesses: dedicated Growth and Express pods, supported by a shared Marketplace Intelligence and Platform layer. That is the structure most likely to let Checkatrade protect today's revenue engine while building the product, data, and operating foundations of its next phase of growth.

A. Context

TL;DR
  • Checkatrade's future is not a better directory; it is a homeowner OS and a trade OS.
  • In an AI-shaped market, defensibility will concentrate in hard-to-replicate supply, higher levels of marketplace management, and higher levels of customer engagement.
  • Checkatrade is strong on supply, weak on marketplace management, and exposed on customer engagement.
  • The strategic answer is in 4 strategic bets: deeper supply advantage, higher level of marketplace management, AI-native matching and routing, and stronger customer engagement through building operating systems for our customers on both sides.

A.1 The next version of Checkatrade is not a better directory - it is a household operating system for homeowners and a growth operating system for trades

“I'm a homeowner in London. Today, when something breaks in my home, I still have to do too much work myself. I need to figure out what kind of trade I even need, describe the issue, compare options, chase responses, judge whether pricing feels fair, and hope I pick someone reliable. In the future, I want Checkatrade to do that work with me. I want to describe the problem once, get guided to the right solution, book with confidence, pay through the platform, and then come back to the same place to manage the rest of my household over time. Not just a plumber today, but my boiler service next month, my garden in spring, and the electrician I trust when I move house. I do not want a directory. I want a household operating system.”
“I'm a tradesperson in Manchester. Today, platforms help me get discovered, but I still spend too much time sorting through poor-fit leads, quoting manually, and trying to turn inconsistent, unstructured demands into a real business. In the future, I want Checkatrade to understand my capabilities, service area, availability, response speed, pricing patterns, and job preferences, then bring me the right work in the right format. I want fewer dead ends, less admin, better conversion, and software that helps me run my business, not just advertise it. I do not want to keep paying for visibility alone. I want a platform that helps me grow.”

That is the strategic shift: from directory to single-player mode on both sides of the marketplace. For homeowners, Checkatrade should become the system that helps manage the household. For trades, it should become the system that helps manage demand, workflow, and business operations.

A.2 In an AI-shaped market, value will concentrate in hard supply, deeper marketplace management, and stronger direct engagement

Marketplaces historically tolerated high CAC because they earned it back through repeat usage, habit, and the economics of the ongoing relationship. If AI becomes the default place customers start, some marketplaces will be forced to reacquire demand transaction by transaction. That compresses margins and weakens repeat behavior.

The winners will be the marketplaces that remain essential because they:

  • aggregate hard-to-replicate supply
  • manage more of the workflow
  • give customers a reason to come back directly

Checkatrade should be assessed through those three lenses.

1. Supply aggregation: strong foundation

Checkatrade is well positioned on supply. Local trade supply is fragmented, heterogeneous, and difficult to make legible. The company already has real advantages in brand, vetting, and scale.

The gap is not access to supply. It is making supply legible in real time: who is right for which job, how likely they are to respond, and what outcome quality to expect.

What I am missing here that might slightly change this view is current supply concentration data.

Takeaway: Checkatrade has a real supply moat, but has not yet turned it into a superior matching and fulfillment advantage.

2. Marketplace management: weakest today, biggest upside

This is the biggest strategic gap. Lead gen is the least defensible end of the spectrum. Managed marketplaces are more defensible because they own more of what customers actually care about: scoping, pricing, trust, payments, and service delivery.

This is also where AI matters most. In home services, demand is messy. Customers often do not know what they need, jobs are hard to scope, and pricing is inconsistent. AI can turn unstructured intent into structured scope, which makes booking, guarantees, and managed fulfillment much more feasible.

Takeaway: marketplace management is Checkatrade's weakest pillar today and its biggest source of future value.

3. Customer engagement: exposed unless Checkatrade broadens its role

Home services is low-frequency and medium/high-consideration. That makes it vulnerable to AI intermediaries unless Checkatrade gives customers a stronger reason to come direct.

The answer is not to force frequency. It is to broaden the platform's role:

  • maintenance
  • planning
  • records
  • guarantees
  • credit
  • rebooking
  • reminders
  • trusted household management

The same applies on the trade side: engagement deepens if Checkatrade becomes part of how the trade runs the business.

Takeaway: Checkatrade is vulnerable if it remains a discovery layer; it is much stronger if it becomes infrastructure for both households and trades.

A.3 The implication: run core and Express as one integrated system

Checkatrade is:

  • strong on supply aggregation
  • underdeveloped on marketplace management
  • exposed on engagement on both sides unless it expands its role

So the right strategy is not to run lead gen and Express as separate businesses. It is to build one system that routes jobs dynamically across service levels:

  • some jobs stay in lead gen
  • some move to guided matching
  • some move to fixed-price or instant-book
  • some become more deeply managed over time

The core business monetizes introductions today. Express is where Checkatrade learns to monetize outcomes.

A.4 The window to act is now: 4 strategic bets

If AI becomes the starting point for home services discovery, the basis of advantage shifts. Checkatrade will not win by being closer to search. It will win by being closer to the outcome.

That leads to four strategic bets:

1. Go deeper with supply by becoming the operating system for tradespeople

Checkatrade should deepen its supply moat by helping trades manage demand, workflow, availability, reputation, pricing signals, and repeat business.

2. Move up the marketplace management stack

The company should do the things AI layers will not do well on their own: guarantees, booking reliability, payments, credit, dispute resolution, trust, and selected managed fulfillment.

3. Build AI natively into the marketplace

AI should be embedded in the product to scope jobs, structure demand, improve routing, normalize pricing expectations, and compress time-to-value.

4. Increase customer engagement and build the operating system for homeowners

Over time, Checkatrade should expand from episodic jobs into broader household management: reminders, records, maintenance, rebooking, and adjacent needs.

B. Findings - Case study Part 1.1

TL;DR
  • Demand likely needs to grow materially to hit the plan.
  • BOs per customer are too low relative to the household opportunity, the product is reactive, not proactive.
  • Low and deteriorating hire-rate is the clearest sign of value leakage in the messy middle.
  • The app is not yet a meaningfully better product surface.
  • The business should increasingly optimize for successful jobs completed, not just BO volume.

B.1 Finding 1: top-of-funnel demand likely needs to grow materially

Checkatrade is targeting:

  • 14% growth in consumer BOs
  • 20% growth in overall revenue
  • 20% growth in non-leads revenue

Against that backdrop, the dummy data shows unique customers declining materially across the year. That may be partly seasonal, but it still raises a clear concern: the business will struggle to hit plan without either stronger demand growth or a meaningfully more efficient funnel.

Two plausible explanations need testing:

  • Seasonality / mix: home services demand may be stronger in colder months or urgent categories (especially if Checkatrade is particularly strong in "winter" categories).
  • Acquisition fragility: Google-led acquisition may be becoming less effective at the margin as discovery fragments.

Conclusion: Either way, Checkatrade likely needs both more demand and a more diversified demand engine. Potentially a more diversified supply/use cases to soften seasonality but on this the case study, I will focus on the demand side as insights I got during the interview process led me to believe that this should be a focus.

B.2 Finding 2: BOs per customer are too low relative to the household opportunity

Homeowners likely have 10–15 home service needs per year across plumbing, electrical, boiler, garden, cleaning, and seasonal maintenance. BOs per customer on Checkatrade are flat and sit well below that. Checkatrade is winning first-job capture but losing wallet share.

The gap is structural, not acquisition-driven. The current product is reactive: it waits for the homeowner to articulate a need rather than surfacing the needs their household is likely to have. Without property context or proactive prompts, every job starts from a cold start.

Conclusion: more traffic will not close this gap. Lifting BOs per customer requires a shift from a reactive directory to a proactive household surface, which is also the precondition for becoming a homeowner OS.

B.3 Finding 3: hire rates are too soft, and the biggest value leak is in the messy middle

Hire rate is the clearest conversion signal in the dataset. It falls materially over the year across web and app. Seasonality may explain part of that, but even allowing for that, the end-state hire rates look soft for what should be relatively high-intent BOs.

This points to the messy middle:

  • poor scoping
  • weak routing
  • slow or inconsistent responses
  • unclear pricing expectations
  • insufficient trust at the point of decision
  • too much operational work left to the homeowners and trades

Conclusion: the biggest improvement opportunity is not just more customers; it is better conversion from BO to hire.

B.4 Finding 4: the app is not yet a meaningfully better product surface

App adoption is growing, which is encouraging. But the app does not yet appear to produce meaningfully better outcomes:

  • BOs per user are flat - in a world where homeowners have likely 10-15 use cases per year, the app does not show that it's contributing in winning homeowners wallet share
  • hire rate broadly tracks web
  • the app does not yet look like the clear home of a more guided, transactable, repeatable experience

Conclusion: the app is an important product gap. If Checkatrade wants to build a managed marketplace and stronger repeat usage, the app should become the clearest expression of that future. While we are succeeding at driving more people to the app, we are failing at providing more value through the app.

C. Recommendations for the Core Business - Case study Part 1.2

TL;DR
  • The core business should do two things over the next 12 months: protect the revenue engine and build the product/data foundations for a more managed marketplace.
  • That requires three priorities initiatives:
    • improve BO quality through better onboarding and intake
    • improve BO-to-hire conversion through better matching
    • Once the app proves better, shift more high-intent users from web into the app
  • Given the limited visibility into the full funnel and cohort dynamics, I am grounding these recommendations in the available data, combined with product intuition and commercial judgment. With deeper funnel and cohort data, prioritization could be further refined.

C.1 Initiative 1: Increase BOs by rebuilding app onboarding and intake as an AI-native problem-to-plan flow

Why

When I download the app, onboarding is close to non existent and my first landing feels very generic/ non personalized. Equally, the intake flow puts too much of the scoping burden on the homeowner. In home services, users often do not know:

  • what trade they need
  • what details matter
  • whether the job is simple, urgent, diagnostic, or complex

Recommendation

Rebuild onboarding and turn intake into a job understanding and routing engine.

Onboarding - For a successful homeowner activation, onboarding is crucial. Asking a few question about the type of property they own, can lead to a more personalized landing tailored to the upkeep of that specific property. If Checkatrade can say:

  • “Homes like yours often need boiler servicing before winter”
  • “Victorian homes in your area commonly need gutter, damp, or roof checks”
  • “You have a garden and spring is coming up - would you like to book a garden tidy?”
  • “Based on your property type, here are common next jobs”

then the platform stops being reactive and becomes more proactive from day 1, minimizing the cold start problem.

BO Intake - Not every job should go through the same path:

  • Fast path: simple, high-intent jobs recommended on the homepage
  • Guided path: ambiguous or higher-consideration jobs guided through AI search
  • Guides: for low-intent homeowners who don't know what trade they need and how to best maintain their property proactively before it gets late/expensive. We can leverage AI to personalize these and cover a high % of use cases

Currently, I land on a non-personalized homescreen with random recommendations and a prompt to "save tradespeople I trust" (even though I have never used the app before) occupying prime real estate at the wrong time. The assistant is a good feature but the entry points for the various journeys to BO are unclear, non-personalized and overwhelming (homescreen, search bar at the top, 2 different entry points to the AI assistant). I would introduce onboarding, and simplify the UX to introduce personalization and focus on the high converting journeys which are likely to be homepage, search (which I would unify under AI search), and guides for lower intent users. The app needs to feel like my homeowner OS from the start.

Crucially, this approach would have to be validated with quant and qual data that I currently do not have access to. This statement holds true for the other initiatives too but I will not repeat it every time.

Hypothesis

If Checkatrade guides users to what they need, helps them articulate the jobs more clearly and routes simple vs complex jobs differently, then we should get more, higher quality BOs.

Success metrics

  • Traffic to BO conversion should increase
  • BOs per customer should increase

C.2 Initiative 2: Increase hire rate by improving app matching and workflow routing

Why

The goal is not to "match better" in the abstract. It is to get each BO into the supplier workflow that can digest it fastest and most effectively.

Recommendation

Create a workflow-aware routing layer.

Classify jobs by digestibility:

  • instant-digest
  • structured quote
  • diagnostic
  • high-consideration / consultative

Then route not just by supplier fit, but by:

  • workflow compatibility
  • urgency
  • need for site visit
  • likely response speed
  • likely conversion quality

Package BOs better for suppliers:

  • structured job briefs
  • clearer urgency signals
  • better quality signals
  • simple action paths: accept, estimate, callback, diagnostic visit, decline

Hypothesis

If Checkatrade routes each BO into the supplier workflow that can absorb it most efficiently, then time-to-hire and hire rate will improve materially.

Minimum viable experiment

Start in a small number of categories:

  • classify BOs into workflow types
  • build routing logic for those types
  • improve trade ranking within each path
  • redesign supplier-facing BO packaging
  • launch lightweight response tools

Success metrics

  • Hire rate should increase
  • Time to hire should decrease
  • Buyer and supplier experience should improve (measured through qual feedback and NPS)

Funny story: I had a very direct reminder of the current product experience while testing Checkatrade. I posted a small job to replace a shower hose and quickly found myself juggling calls, texts, and emails from multiple trades. For a simple fix, it felt like a lot. When I admitted on one call that I was only testing the flow and did not actually need the job done, the tradesperson gave me a well-deserved telling-off and reminded me that they pay for those leads. It was funny, but it also made the problem very clear: too much noise for the homeowner, too much wasted effort for the trade. Better scoping, matching, and routing would improve both sides of that interaction.

C.3 Initiative 3: Make the app the default platform for high-intent users once it proves superior economics

Why

Initiatives 1 and 2 are designed to make the app meaningfully better: better onboarding, better BO creation, better routing, and better job management. Once that foundation is in place, Checkatrade should not treat web and app as equivalent surfaces. It should deliberately shift more high-intent users into the app.

This matters because the app has the potential to become a structurally better platform for both conversion and retention. It can support:

  • more personalized onboarding
  • better BO creation and job management
  • richer trust and communication flows
  • stronger lifecycle and repeat usage
  • lower future acquisition costs through direct engagement

Today, the app does not yet clearly outperform web. But if it begins to show:

  • higher traffic-to-BO conversion
  • higher hire rate
  • higher repeat usage
  • higher LTV per user

then moving more high-intent demand into the app becomes a strategic priority, not just a growth tactic.

Recommendation

Treat web as the broad discovery and capture layer, and the app as the default operating environment for high-intent homeowners.

The shift should happen only once the app proves superior economics in selected journeys and categories. At that point, Checkatrade should deliberately migrate users into the app at moments where persistence, trust, and repeat interaction matter most.

The goal is not to maximize app downloads. It is to migrate the right users into the right surface.

The highest-value moments for migration are likely to be:

  • immediately after first BO creation
  • when a homeowner starts receiving responses and needs to compare options
  • when messaging, tracking, or trust matter more
  • after job completion, when rebooking, records, or reminders become relevant

The strategic logic is simple:

  • web is the acquisition and discovery layer
  • app becomes the conversion, trust, and retention layer

That is how Checkatrade starts turning episodic traffic into a more durable homeowner relationship.

Hypothesis

If Checkatrade makes the app meaningfully better than web for high-intent journeys, and then deliberately shifts those users into the app at the right moments, then BO conversion, hire rate, and LTV per user will improve because the app is a better surface for trust, persistence, and repeat engagement.

Minimum viable experiment

I would test this in the same priority categories used in initiatives 1 and 2.

The first wave should focus on a few high-intent migration points:

  • prompt web users to continue in app after BO creation
  • move quote / response management into the app
  • make job tracking and messaging more valuable in app
  • introduce app-specific persistence such as saved job history, reminders, and easier rebooking

The goal of the MVP is not to maximize install rate. It is to prove that users who move into the app perform better than comparable users who stay on web.

Success metrics

I would measure this initiative on:

  • web-to-app migration rate
  • BO conversion rate for migrated users vs web-only users
  • hire rate for migrated users vs web-only users
  • repeat usage / rebooking rate
  • LTV per migrated user

Capital allocation

This should remain the third priority, but only after the app begins to prove better economics in the core journeys improved by initiatives 1 and 2.

The sequencing matters:

  • first, make the app better
  • second, prove it converts and retains better
  • third, shift more high-intent users into it

This is not a channel tactic. It is a platform shift. The app should earn the right to become the primary surface for high-intent homeowners by outperforming web on conversion, retention, and lifetime value.

C.4 Why these bets matter beyond the core business

These are core-business initiatives, but they also build the infrastructure for Express:

  • Initiative 1 creates more structured, higher-quality demand and determines the right service path.
  • Initiative 2 improves matching, routing, and supplier-side conversion, creating the bridge between lead gen and marketplace.
  • Initiative 3 turns the app into the default platform for high-intent homeowners once it proves better economics, strengthening trust, retention, and the path to a homeowner operating system.

This is not "optimize the old business." It is the first stage of rebuilding Checkatrade as a smarter, more transactable marketplace.

D. Managed Marketplace / Express: 12-Month Growth Strategy - Case study Part 2

TL;DR
  • Assuming we are starting from zero - scale Express supply-first inside existing demand pools.
  • Start in constrained categories and dense cities; prove reliability before breadth.
  • Design the product to reduce disintermediation from day one.
  • Organize Express as a real business pod, not a side project.

D.1 Express should be treated as the wedge into a more managed marketplace

Express matters because it is how Checkatrade learns to monetize outcomes, not just introductions.

The goal over the next 12 months is to prove that, in selected categories, a managed marketplace can deliver, then scale:

  • faster time-to-outcome
  • higher hire rates
  • higher frequency of use
  • stronger unit economics

D.2 GTM strategy - Case study Part 2.1

Recommendation

The following recommendation is based on the case-study assumption that Express is being launched from zero. In practice, I would not apply a blanket "supply first" rule across the board. I would assess each market at the category × city level, because the right GTM depends on where the constraint sits. Some markets will be healthy, with enough demand and enough supply, in which case the focus should be on routing, conversion, and proving the managed value proposition. Some will be supply-constrained, with strong demand but insufficient density or workflow-ready trades, in which case the priority should be targeted supply acquisition, activation, and possibly short-term supplier incentives to improve customer experience. Others will be demand-constrained, where supply exists but demand is too thin, in which case the better move is to increase demand through better routing from the core business, selective acquisition, lifecycle reactivation, or adjacent-job generation rather than over-investing in more supply. Finally, some market cells will simply be unhealthy, with low demand and low supply, and those should not be early Express priorities at all. So while "supply first" (and leveraging existing demand) is the right default for a zero-to-one launch, the real operating model should be much more granular: diagnose the health of each category x city, identify whether demand or supply is the bottleneck, and then choose the GTM motion accordingly.

For the sake of this case study, if existing demand and supply which is a good fit for Express use cases exist, leverage them. If not, prioritize supply first within existing demand pools.

That means:

  • start with existing Checkatrade users, trades, and categories where possible
  • focus on 1-3 dense geographies
  • launch in a small number of highly digestible categories
  • build the operating model there first
  • expand only once hire rates, reliability, and contribution economics are proven

Why supply first

Leverage the existing user base and homeowners acquisition engine for the core business (assuming this can generate enough relevant demand for the selected categories to test) and focus on acquiring trades that are fit for purpose for the Express test (eg high urgency and frequency use cases). If these types of trades are already available on the core business, then we should start with a proof of concept that introduces new use cases leveraging existing demand and existing supply. Basically keep both sides of the marketplace stable, then start expanding one side at a time (supply first).

Category criteria

Start in categories that are:

  • urgent or relatively frequent – creates repeat use, drives density, and surfaces supply/demand mismatches quickly
  • narrow enough to standardize – the job can be defined, priced, and quality-checked against a consistent template
  • easy enough to scope remotely – the homeowner can describe the job, send a photo, or answer a few structured questions, and the trade can commit without a site visit first. Essential for a fast, managed promise
  • operationally manageable – Checkatrade can actually run the end-to-end operating layer (dispatch, SLAs, QA, complaints, payments) without every job turning into a bespoke escalation
  • attractive enough to support a take rate – unit economics leave enough margin for a transaction fee after trade payout

Likely candidates:

  • plumbing fixes
  • electrical callouts
  • boiler / heating subcategories
  • drainage / unblocks
  • cleaning
  • gardening
  • selected handyman jobs

Avoid starting with bespoke, consultative projects like major renovations.

Assumption

This strategy assumes the current demand engine can generate enough relevant demand for the selected categories. If that proves false, demand acquisition for Express will need to play a larger role earlier.

D.3 Marketing & Growth Engine: Increase customers by building a diversified, payback-disciplined demand engine - Case study Part 2.2a

Recommendation

For Express, I would allocate capital with one clear principle:

Use channels that let Checkatrade capture high-intent demand efficiently, while staying disciplined enough not to scale faster than supply quality and fulfillment reliability allow.

So this is not a "spend across every channel" strategy. It is a staged capital allocation plan designed to:

  • build early momentum
  • prove the managed value proposition
  • avoid inefficient acquisition before the product and supply side are ready

Because I do not yet have the supply/demand health data by category × geography, I am giving a demand allocation framework based on the interview context and case materials. In practice, I would refine this by city and category depending on where the real bottleneck sits.

My capital allocation bias

If I were allocating incremental marketing capital for Express, my default mix would be:

  • Core-to-Express routing + CRM / direct / app reactivation: highest immediate focus
  • Organic demand capture (SEO, AI search, content, organic social): first major external growth lever
  • Brand / PR / trust-led awareness: sustained supporting investment
  • Influencer / affiliate / partnership tests: selective pilot budget
  • Paid search / performance media: tightly constrained, scaled only where payback is proven

I would explain the mix this way:

1. Start with owned demand: core-to-Express routing, CRM, app, and direct reactivation

This should be the first and cheapest source of momentum.

Before spending heavily on external channels, Checkatrade should use:

  • existing traffic
  • existing homeowner relationships
  • CRM
  • app/web surfaces
  • lifecycle prompts

to push the right jobs into Express.

Why:

  • lowest CAC
  • fastest learning loop
  • easiest way to compare Express vs lead gen performance
  • best way to avoid scaling a marketplace before supply is ready

This is not just a launch tactic. It is the cleanest way to seed the marketplace with high-intent demand while learning where the managed value proposition is strongest.

2. Make organic the first major external growth lever

If the product and supply side are working, this would be my first scale channel.

But "organic" should be defined broadly:

  • Google SEO
  • AI search / answer-engine discoverability
  • organic social
  • over time, referrals if the product becomes strong enough to support them

The key shift is from keyword research to query-universe research: understanding how users describe household problems across search, social, and LLMs before they are ready to hire someone.

That should feed a scalable content engine built through agentic workflows. The goal is not generic SEO volume. It is high-intent, structured content that captures real household needs and routes users into better BO creation flows.

The reason I would bias toward organic is simple: AI has changed the economics of going after the long tail. More queries are now economically worth serving, especially if Checkatrade can create content and landing flows efficiently.

3. Maintain and selectively increase brand / PR investment

I would not treat brand as soft spend. In a more AI-mediated world, it becomes more important, not less.

As discovery compresses into fewer surfaced options, trusted brands gain disproportionate advantage. In home services, brand does more than create awareness:

  • it increases confidence at the moment of hire
  • strengthens direct demand over time
  • makes users more willing to let the platform manage more of the workflow

So I would continue investing in:

  • PR
  • trust-building creative content
  • category education
  • selected awareness channels such as TV and, where justified, OOH
  • stronger brand-to-demand integration

The key is to measure brand properly and treat it as a multiplier on demand efficiency, not a disconnected top-of-funnel budget.

4. Run trust-led channel tests: influencers, affiliates, partnerships

These should be pilot channels, not immediate scale bets.

I would selectively test:

  • influencers
  • affiliates
  • partnerships

These channels are attractive because home services is a trust-sensitive category. In the right categories, creator-led or partner-led acquisition may outperform more transactional channels. Influencers are also gaining importance again in an AI-shaped world because user-generated content and trusted third-party commentary are increasingly surfaced in AI discovery flows.

If these channels start to work, I would then use agentic workflows to reduce the cost of operating them:

  • creator / partner identification
  • segmentation
  • outreach
  • offer testing
  • Activation
  • Re-activation

That matters because these channels often fail economically when they are run manually at too small a scale.

5. Re-underwrite paid media rather than assume it should scale

Paid should be treated as a portfolio, not a default growth answer.

Some categories may still justify meaningful paid investment:

  • urgent jobs
  • highly monetizable jobs
  • categories with strong conversion and clear supply readiness

Others may be overfunded or structurally less resilient.

So the right question is not "should we use PPC?" It is:

  • where should we double down
  • where should we hold flat
  • where should we test
  • where should we pull back

I would avoid using paid to cover up weak supply density or poor fulfillment. That is how marketplaces burn capital and damage the customer experience.

Suggested staged allocation

Without granular city/category data, I would not pretend to know the exact mix. But my default staged mix would look something like:

The case study assumes we are launching from scratch. In reality, initially there should be a phase 0 to diagnose and identify where the next marginal pound should go based on the current core business growth engine,

Phase 1: launch and learn

  • 40–50% owned demand: routing from core, CRM, app, direct
  • 20–25% organic / content / AI-search capture
  • 15–20% brand / PR / trust messaging
  • 5–10% influencer / affiliate / partnership pilots
  • 10–15% selective paid search

Phase 2: once supply readiness and conversion are proven

Shift more capital toward:

  • organic and brand if they are compounding efficiently
  • selective paid in categories where payback is proven
  • scaling influencers / affiliates only if the pilots show strong unit economics

I would not lock into these percentages without better data, but this is the directional bias I would take.

Hypothesis

If Checkatrade builds a diversified Express demand portfolio, starting with owned demand, then scaling organic, brand, and selected trust-led channels, while keeping paid tightly constrained, then it can build momentum efficiently without outrunning supply readiness or diluting payback.

How I would measure success

I would measure this initiative on:

  • Unique-customers increase
  • CAC decreases
  • Payback by channel and category improves

The key is not just volume. It is whether each channel is producing qualified demand that the marketplace can fulfill profitably and reliably.

Bottom line

My bias would be:

  • first dollars into owned demand, measurement, and organic capture
  • second dollars into brand preservation and selective scaling
  • pilot dollars into influencers / affiliates / partnerships
  • paid scaled only where payback is proven

In other words: do not try to buy momentum before the product and supply side are ready. First prove the economics of each managed job. Then scale the channels that can add demand without breaking them.

D.4 Sustainable growth loop: a data network effect - Case study Part 2.2b

Recommendation

The growth loop I am proposing is, fundamentally, a data network effect: every successful job makes the platform more intelligent, which makes the next job better for both sides, which drives more volume, which produces more data. Build it around: Successful jobs completed > better trust and performance data > better household intelligence and routing > more repeat usage and referrals on both sides > lower paid dependence

Successful jobs should generate:

  • better supplier performance data
  • better pricing / duration / conversion benchmarks
  • better understanding of the household and likely future needs

Then use AI-first homeowner profiling and household memory to make repeat usage easier.

A lightweight home profile should get richer over time:

  • property type
  • age of home
  • boiler / heating / roof / windows / drainage context
  • previous jobs
  • recurring needs
  • likely future maintenance

This enables:

  • better personalization
  • proactive reminders
  • easier repeat booking
  • smarter routing
  • lower transaction cost on future jobs

On the other side of the marketplace, better trade workflows should lead to suppliers wanting to do more jobs on the Checkatrade OS, which should become more convenient that doing it off platform.

This is the data network effect at work: the platform becomes more useful with every job completed, ideally on both sides of the marketplace, which is what unlocks the full power of referrals. Referrals should flow buyer to buyer (homeowners recommending the platform to other homeowners) and, where the OS value proposition is strong enough on the supply side, seller to buyer too (trades actively pointing their own customers back into Checkatrade because doing the job on-platform is more convenient than off it). This is also one of the strongest defenses against disintermediation.
